Introduction

자기소개

Q. Could you briefly introduce yourself?

I'm the CEO and Co-Founder of SelaNet.ai, where we're building trusted infrastructure that enables AI agents to operate freely on the web. With over a decade of experience across finance and blockchain, I've witnessed firsthand how infrastructure transforms markets — and now I'm channeling that experience into the AI agent ecosystem.

Q. How would you describe what you do in one sentence?

We're building trusted infrastructure that lets AI agents open browsers, navigate websites, log in, and even make payments — all autonomously, without human intervention. Today's web is designed entirely for humans: CAPTCHAs, cookie consents, complex UIs. For agents, every one of these is a barrier. SelaNet.ai tears down those barriers to make agents first-class citizens of the web.

AI Agent & Browser Automation

Q. Why are you so focused on AI agent infrastructure?

Most AI we see today is trapped inside chat windows. But the real value emerges when agents step out of the chat and take action on the real web — searching flights, comparing prices, booking tickets, logging into SaaS tools, pulling reports. For this to work, agents need to autonomously operate browsers, and websites need to trust those agents. Both problems must be solved simultaneously, and no one has cracked it yet.

Q. Why is browser automation the key to AI agents?

Only a tiny fraction of services have APIs. 99% of the web is still accessible only through browsers. For agents to be genuinely useful, they need to freely navigate and interact with websites that have no API. Tools like Playwright and Puppeteer laid the foundation, and now AI is evolving to combine visual understanding with decision-making on top of them. But there's a missing piece — trust and authentication. We need infrastructure that can distinguish between a malicious bot and a trusted agent. That's exactly the problem SelaNet.ai is solving.

Q. How do you see the current AI agent market?

It's on the verge of explosion. OpenAI's Operator, Anthropic's Computer Use, Google's Project Mariner — every big tech company is betting on agents operating on the web. But they're all focused on 'how smart the agent is.' The question of 'what infrastructure is needed for agents to be trusted on the web' remains largely unexplored territory. That's where we're digging in. As agents get smarter, the demand for infrastructure will grow exponentially.

Q. What specific problems does SelaNet.ai solve?

Three things. First, Agent Identity — proving who owns the agent and what permissions it has. Second, Trusted Browser Sessions — guaranteeing that an agent's browser activity is secure and verifiable. Third, Agent Interaction Protocol — a standard that allows different agents to safely collaborate and transact on the web. This is an infrastructure problem on the same level as SSL/HTTPS enabling e-commerce in the early internet.

Blockchain & DePIN

블록체인 & DePIN

Q. How does your blockchain experience connect to AI agent infrastructure?

At its core, blockchain is about enabling trusted transactions between untrusted parties. The exact same problem arises in the AI agent world. Will the agent I sent behave correctly on someone else's website? Can I trust the response from another agent? Trustless verification, cryptographic proofs, distributed consensus — every concept blockchain solved applies directly to agent infrastructure. Seven years of blockchain experience at BC Labs is deeply woven into SelaNet.ai's architecture.

Q. What sparked your interest in DePIN (Decentralized Physical Infrastructure Networks)?

For AI agents to autonomously operate browsers, they need computing resources. Concentrating everything in a single cloud creates a single point of failure and exploding costs. DePIN is the answer — a global network of distributed nodes hosting agent browser sessions in exchange for token incentives. It's about decentralizing the 'physical layer' of agent infrastructure. Running Coinvestor gave me deep understanding of crypto incentive mechanisms, which has been directly instrumental in designing the DePIN model.

Q. What are the most exciting trends at the intersection of blockchain and AI?

Three trends stand out. First, Agent-to-Agent Payment — agents exchanging services through instant crypto payments. Second, Verifiable Compute — using zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) to verify that an agent's work hasn't been tampered with. Third, DePIN-based browser networks — agents running on globally distributed nodes, each powered by economic incentives. When these three converge, autonomous economic activity by agents becomes possible. I'm convinced this will form the foundation of the 'Agentic Economy.'

Q. What was the most important lesson from Coinvestor?

The value of data infrastructure. Coinvestor was a crypto hedge fund marketplace, and over four years of operation, the hottest demand wasn't for fund products — it was for data. Quant managers were desperate for on-chain data, social signals, and exchange API data, and that demand kept growing more diverse and massive. That's when it clicked — the next era's killer app would be agents that can autonomously collect, process, and leverage data, and those agents absolutely need infrastructure to freely navigate the web.

Q. From finance to blockchain to AI — was this transition natural?

Surprisingly natural. Finance is ultimately about 'transactions built on trust.' Blockchain was an attempt to replace that trust with code and cryptography. AI agent infrastructure extends the subject of trust from 'humans' to 'machines.' The technology changed every time, but the essence of the problem I'm solving has always been the same — 'How do you create trusted transactions in an untrusted environment?'

Q. What's the hardest technical challenge in agent infrastructure?

Redefining our relationship with anti-bot systems. The web has treated automated access as the enemy. CAPTCHAs, rate limiting, fingerprinting — all built to block bots. But in the AI agent era, this paradigm must shift: from 'block all automation' to 'allow trusted agents.' This isn't a pure technology problem — it's about redesigning the entire web's authentication framework. That's what makes it both the hardest and the most fascinating challenge.

Q. Why is the DePIN model necessary for AI agent infrastructure?

For agents to operate on the web, browsers need to run 'somewhere.' Relying solely on centralized clouds like AWS creates three problems: cost concentration, single points of failure, and regional access limitations. DePIN is a network where globally distributed nodes host browser sessions. Each node operator provides computing resources and earns token incentives. Agents run on the nearest, cheapest node. Blockchain's 'decentralized incentive mechanism' solves the physical infrastructure problem. My experience designing crypto incentives at Coinvestor has been directly instrumental in conceptualizing this model.

Vision

비전

Q. What will AI agent web activity look like in 5 years?

Everyone will have a personal agent. These agents will compare prices, make reservations, submit documents, and process payments on the web on your behalf. Enterprises will run hundreds of agents to automate competitor monitoring, lead generation, and customer service. Agent-to-Agent Commerce — where agents directly negotiate and transact with each other — will emerge. For all of this to work, there needs to be infrastructure for agents to be authenticated, trusted, and able to transact on the web. SelaNet.ai is building that infrastructure.

Q. What kind of world do you ultimately want to create?

The Agentic Web — a world where agents become first-class citizens of the web. In the 1990s, the web was for reading documents. In the 2000s, it became a place for transactions. In the 2010s, social platforms. From the late 2020s onward, the web evolves into a space where agents operate. Just as SSL unlocked e-commerce in that transition, I want SelaNet.ai to become the trust layer that unlocks the agent economy.
